# Introduction to Trading Strategy Development

A trading strategy is a systematic method for identifying trade opportunities and managing positions. Developing a profitable strategy requires understanding market mechanics, rigorous testing, and continuous refinement. This is the foundation of successful trading.

## What Makes a Trading Strategy

A complete trading strategy includes:
- **Market selection**: Which markets to trade
- **Entry criteria**: When to enter positions
- **Position sizing**: How much to risk
- **Exit rules**: When to take profits or losses
- **Time frames**: Which time frames to use

Without all components, a strategy is incomplete.

## Types of Trading Strategies

### Technical Strategies
Technical strategies use price and volume data:
- **Trend following**: Trade in the direction of trends
- **Mean reversion**: Trade away from averages
- **Breakout trading**: Trade new highs or lows
- **Pattern recognition**: Trade chart formations

### Fundamental Strategies
Fundamental strategies use economic data:
- **Value investing**: Buy underpriced securities
- **Growth investing**: Buy high-growth companies
- **Momentum investing**: Buy strong performers
- **Event trading**: Trade on news events

### Quantitative Strategies
Quantitative strategies use mathematical models:
- **Statistical arbitrage**: Exploit price inefficiencies
- **Pairs trading**: Trade correlated instruments
- **Machine learning**: Use predictive models
- **Multi-factor models**: Combine multiple signals

### Sentiment Strategies
Sentiment strategies use market情绪:
- **Contrarian trading**: Trade against consensus
- **News trading**: Trade on headlines
- **Insider trading**: Follow insider activity
- **Option sentiment**: Use options data

## Strategy Development Process

### 1. Idea Generation
Start with observations:
- What market patterns appear repeatedly?
- What has worked historically?
- What do other traders discuss?
- What economic relationships exist?

Ideas can come from research, observation, or inspiration.

### 2. Hypothesis Formation
Form specific hypotheses:
- "Price breaks above the 50-day moving average leads to higher prices"
- "Earnings beats lead to post-earnings price increases"
- "Low volatility leads to volatility expansion"

Clear hypotheses enable testing.

### 3. Defining Rules
Convert hypotheses to rules:
- **Entry rules**: Specific, testable conditions
- **Exit rules**: Profit targets and stop levels
- **Position sizing**: Deterministic sizing method
- **Filters**: Market conditions to avoid

Precise rules enable backtesting.

### 4. Initial Testing
Test hypotheses quickly:
- Manual backtesting on charts
- Backtesting software
- Conservative assumptions
- Multiple market conditions

Initial testing reveals viability.

### 5. Refinement
Refine based on testing:
- Adjust entry parameters
- Modify exit rules
- Add filters
- Optimize position sizing

Refinement improves performance.

### 6. Forward Testing
Test on current market data:
- Paper trading
- Simulated trading
- Small live capital

Forward testing reveals real-world viability.

### 7. Live Deployment
Deploy with caution:
- Small capital initially
- Full risk controls
- Close monitoring
- Gradual scaling

## Key Strategy Components

### Entry Criteria
Clear entry rules specify:
- Market conditions required
- Technical requirements
- Confirmation requirements
- Timing considerations

### Position Sizing
Deterministic sizing provides:
- Calculated position sizes
- Consistent risk per trade
- Maximum drawdown control

### Risk Management
Risk management includes:
- Stop-loss placement
- Maximum position limits
- Daily loss limits
- Correlation awareness

### Exit Strategy
Exits include:
- Take-profit levels
- Stop-loss levels
- Time-based exits
- Discretionary exits

## Common Strategy Mistakes

### Over-Complexity
Complex strategies fail:
- Too many indicators
- Too many rules
- Hard to execute
- Difficult to understand

Keep strategies simple and executable.

### Curve Fitting
Over-optimization destroys:
- Optimizing to historical noise
- Poor forward performance
- False confidence
- Unexpected live results

Use realistic parameters and out-of-sample testing.

### Lack of Edge
Without an edge, strategies fail:
- Strategies must have positive expectancy
- Simple ideas often work best
- Complexity doesn't guarantee edge
- Test thoroughly before trading

### Ignoring Costs
Costs destroy profitability:
- Commissions compound
- Slippage matters
- Spread costs accumulate
- Account for realistic costs

## Developing Your Own Strategy

### Start Simple
Begin with basics:
- Single indicator or concept
- Clear entry and exit
- Test thoroughly
- Add complexity only if needed

### Use What You Know
Build on expertise:
- Industry knowledge
- Technical skills
- Professional experience
- Personal interests

### Copy Then Customize
Learn from others:
- Study existing strategies
- Understand the logic
- Customize to personal style
- Test thoroughly

### Document Everything
Document provides:
- Clear rules
- Reproduction capability
- Refinement ability
- Troubleshooting

## Testing Considerations

### Historical Testing
Historical tests reveal:
- Past performance
- Parameter sensitivity
- Market conditions
- Drawdown potential

### Sample Size
Adequate samples require:
- Sufficient trades for significance
- Multiple market conditions
- Various time periods
- Different instruments

### Realistic Assumptions
Realistic testing includes:
- Slippage estimates
- Commission costs
- Partial fills
- Market impact

## Conclusion

Strategy development requires:
- Clear hypotheses
- Precise rules
- Rigorous testing
- Continuous refinement

The goal is a simple, executable strategy with demonstrated historical performance and realistic expectations. Success comes from consistency and discipline in following a proven strategy.

Develop strategies systematically. Test thoroughly. Trade consistently.
