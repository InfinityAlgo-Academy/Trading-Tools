# Introduction to Risk Management

Risk management is the foundation of successful trading. Without proper risk management, even the most profitable strategy will eventually destroy a trading account. Many traders who possess winning strategies still fail because they neglect this critical discipline.

## The Primary Rule: Preserve Capital

The first objective of every trader should be capital preservation. Without capital, trading becomes impossible. This simple principle guides all risk management decisions.

Traders who lose too much capital face an impossible recovery. A 50% loss requires a 100% gain to break even. A 90% loss requires a 900% gain—an nearly impossible achievement. This mathematics demonstrates why protecting capital matters more than chasing profits.

## Understanding Risk-Reward Ratio

The risk-reward ratio measures the potential reward compared to the risked amount. A ratio of 1:2 means risking $1 to potentially earn $2. This ratio is fundamental to profitable trading.

Even with a strategy that wins only 40% of trades, a 1:2 risk-reward ratio produces profitability:
- Win 4 trades: 4 × $2 = $8 profit
- Lose 6 trades: 6 × $1 = $6 loss
- Net result: $2 profit

This calculation reveals why professional traders prioritize risk-reward ratios over win rates.

## Position Sizing

Position sizing determines how much capital to risk on any single trade. The standard rule risks no more than 1-2% of total capital per trade. This limit ensures that a series of losses won't devastatingly impact the account.

For example, with a $10,000 account and 2% maximum risk per trade:
- Maximum risk per trade = $200
- If risking $100 per point, maximum position = 2 points

This discipline allows traders to survive losing streaks while remaining in the game.

## Stop-Loss Orders

Stop-loss orders automatically close positions when price reaches a predetermined level. They represent the most important risk management tool.

Effective stop-loss placement considers:
- Technical levels (support, resistance, moving averages)
- Market volatility (ATR-based stops)
- Time-based exits (intraday limits)

Stops should be placed at levels that invalidate the trading thesis, not at arbitrary distances.

## Maximum Daily and Weekly Loss Limits

Beyond per-trade risk, traders should establish maximum loss limits for longer periods:
- Daily maximum: Typically 3-5% of account
- Weekly maximum: Typically 6-10% of account

When reaching these limits, stop trading for the day or week. Emotional recovery requires time, and continuing after reaching limits typically leads to further losses.

##Correlation and Diversification

Trading multiple correlated positions increases overall risk. Positions in the same direction on correlated instruments effectively create larger positions. Understanding correlation helps manage portfolio-level risk.

Diversification across uncorrelated strategies or instruments reduces individual position risk. However, true diversification requires instruments that don't move together during market stress.

## Maximum Position Limits

Traders should establish maximum position limits:
- Maximum positions open simultaneously
- Maximum exposure to any single market or sector
- Maximum correlation-weighted exposure

These limits prevent over-concentration and manage systemic risk.

## The 2% Rule in Practice

The 2% rule provides a practical framework:
1. Calculate 2% of account balance
2. This equals maximum risk per trade
3. Determine stop-loss distance based on technical analysis
4. Calculate position size accordingly

If the calculated position size is too small to execute practically, either widen the stop slightly or wait for better setups.

## Conclusion

Risk management is not optional—it is the trading business model. Without it, even excellent strategies fail. With proper risk management, average strategies can succeed.

The goal is survival through consistency. Following risk management rules ensures traders can return another day to continue exploiting their edge.

Master risk management before mastering any strategy. It is the foundation upon which all trading success builds.
